1. The "Trade-off" Acknowledgment (Step 2)
Explicitly stating en_core_web_trf (high accuracy, high compute) vs. en_core_web_md (good accuracy, low compute) is crucial. In a real-world pipeline where you might need to process 100k JDs overnight, having the md model as a documented fallback strategy saves you from architecture panic later.
2. The 12-Token Span Limit (Step 4)
This is a brilliant, hard-earned lesson in NLP. When extracting dependency trees (verb + dobj + prep + pobj), the tree can easily sprawl into a 30-word run-on sentence. Capping the span at ~12 tokens ensures you extract concise, meaningful actions ("Architected scalable cloud infrastructure using AWS") instead of extracting half a paragraph of noise.
3. The "Low-Signal" Filter (Step 5)
Filtering out words like "appear", "seem", "become", and "get" is essential for job descriptions. You only want actionable verbs that describe responsibilities, not state-of-being or passive verbs. This drastically cleans up your queue.
4. The Tiered Matching Precision (Step 6)
Your thresholds are tuned perfectly for this domain:
Levenshtein ≥ 88%: High enough to catch typos ("architcted") but strict enough to avoid matching completely different short words.
Cosine ≥ 0.82 on MiniLM: This is the sweet spot for sentence-transformers. It’s tight enough to ensure semantic similarity without being so strict that it misses obvious synonyms.
5. The Contextual Seniority Re-weighting (Step 7)
This is the most powerful feature for JD analysis. A junior dev "builds" a feature; a staff engineer "builds" a cross-functional strategy. Hardcoding the context boosters (strategy, P&L, governance) and penalties (routine, administrative) allows you to accurately score seniority even if the base verb ("build") is ambiguous.
6. The Promotion Criteria (Step 8)
Requiring ≥12 occurrences + ≥3 different job families/companies before considering a novel verb for promotion is the ultimate safeguard against scraper noise or one company’s weird internal jargon polluting your taxonomy.
🔍 Subtle "Watch-Outs" for Implementation
While the spec is phenomenal, keep an eye on these edge cases as you write the code:
1. The "Control Verb" Trap (Step 4)
You mentioned grabbing xcomp/ccomp children. Be careful with verbs like "help" or "assist".
Example: "Help design the new API."
If you just grab the root, you get "Help" (low signal). The actual action is "design". You'll need specific logic to bypass control verbs and promote their xcomp children to the primary action if the root verb is weak.
2. The Danger of Aggressive Lemmatization (Step 3)
While .lemma_.lower() is generally correct, sometimes the specific tense matters in a JD, particularly distinguishing between past experience required vs. future responsibilities.
"Has managed teams" (Past/Requirement) vs. "Will manage a team" (Future/Responsibility).
Advice: Keep the raw span alongside the lemma in your metadata (which you wisely noted in Step 9) so you don't lose the temporal context if you need it later.
3. The Cosine Similarity Blind Spot (Step 6/8)
As mentioned previously, all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is great, but embedding models notoriously struggle with antonyms because they appear in identical contexts.
"Increase revenue" and "Decrease revenue" will have a very high cosine similarity.
Advice: When clustering novels (Step 8), rely on the human review step to catch these semantic opposites before they get merged into the same taxonomy group.
4. The HDBSCAN Clustering (Step 8)
HDBSCAN is excellent for finding dense clusters of novel verbs, but it will also output a lot of "noise" (data points that don't fit into any cluster). Don't try to force the noise into taxonomy groups. Let the sparse novel verbs stay pending until they reach critical mass.
